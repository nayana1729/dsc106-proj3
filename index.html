Project Write-Up

Motivation

Our visualization enables users to explore how environmental conditions on a specific calendar date have shifted over time. Climate data can often feel overwhelmingly massive and impersonal. By allowing users to input a highly personal date—such as their birthday—and dynamically toggle between environmental variables, we transform abstract global climate projections into a tangible, personalized historical narrative.

Design Decisions & Visual Encodings

We utilized a connected scatter plot (line chart) as our primary visualization, as it is the most effective visual encoding for expressing continuous temporal trends. The design choices were heavily focused on clarity and narrative:

  • Marks & Channels: The x-axis (horizontal position) encodes the Year, and the y-axis (vertical position) encodes the selected climate variable's value. We applied a Catmull-Rom curve to the lines to smoothly express the continuous nature of environmental data.
  • Color Palette: We used a semantically resonant color scheme. Historical data is rendered in a deep blue, representing established records. The projected scenarios diverge visually: SSP2-4.5 continues in a lighter blue, while SSP5-8.5 diverges sharply in red to draw attention to extreme outcomes.
  • Interactive Context: We encoded specific life milestones (+10, +20, +30 years) as point markers along the path, dynamically calculating the difference between the user's birth year and the modern day to provide immediate, context-heavy details on demand.

Data Transformations

We utilized the CMIP6 (Coupled Model Intercomparison Project) dataset, specifically targeting the MPI-ESM1-2-LR model for daily environmental variables. Because the raw Zarr data on Google Cloud is exceptionally large, querying and filtering spatial arrays dynamically in the browser was infeasible. To optimize the interaction, we transformed the data beforehand: we filtered the historical and projected tables down to global spatial averages, subsetting the data by specific days of the year, and exporting them as highly compressed CSV files. February 29th was excluded due to data sparsity across models.

Development Process

The development was divided iteratively among the team to ensure rapid prototyping and debugging. The work was split as follows:

  • Nayana Naineni: D3.js core charting, line animations, and milestone marker calculations.
  • Ananya Wasker: Interactive event listeners, data state management, and dropdown filtering logic.
  • Jookyoung Lee: UI dashboard architecture, CSS styling, and design documentation.

We spent approximately 18-20 people-hours on this project. The most time-consuming aspect was data wrangling—navigating Google Cloud authentication and formatting the raw CMIP6 spatial data into a structure that D3.js could quickly render without freezing the user's browser.
