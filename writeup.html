Project Write-Up

DSC 106 • Interactive Visualization

Motivation

Climate data is usually massive and hard to relate to. For this project, we wanted to make global climate projections feel personal. By letting users put in their actual birthday and toggle different environmental variables, the visualization turns abstract numbers into a personal history of how the climate has changed since 1850 and where it's going by the year 2100.

Rationale for Design Decisions

Visual Encodings & Alternatives Considered:
For our main visualization, we went with a line chart (connected scatter plot). We thought about using heatmaps to show daily changes, or stacked area charts for the projections. However, heatmaps make it really hard to compare exact numbers across different decades, and stacked area charts didn't make sense here because our future scenarios (SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5) are separate possible futures, not cumulative totals you add together. To make the data look a bit more natural, we smoothed the lines out using a Catmull-Rom curve.

Color Palette:
We wanted the colors to be intuitive. The historical data is a deep blue since it represents the established baseline. For the projections, we used green for SSP2-4.5 (the more moderate emissions path) and a sharp, contrasting red for SSP5-8.5 (the high emissions path) so the extreme outcomes immediately jump out at you.

Interaction Design

Each interactive element maps directly to the Yi et al. (2007) interaction taxonomy covered in lecture, turning a static chart into an exploratory dashboard:

  • Connect ("show me related items"): As you move your mouse horizontally across the chart, a vertical crosshair snaps to the nearest year, and the tooltip shows the values for all active scenarios at once. This links the lines together by time, so you don't have to bounce your eyes back and forth to the y-axis to compare them.
  • Filter ("show me something conditionally"): The scenario buttons on the side let you filter what's shown. You can toggle the historical line or either of the future pathways on and off to isolate just one or compare two. The variable buttons (Temperature, Precipitation, etc.) act as a second filter to completely swap out the dataset.
  • Abstract/Elaborate ("show me more or less detail"): The callout box on the left gives you details-on-demand. While the main chart gives the big picture (abstract), the callout elaborates by showing the exact net change in that specific variable from your birth year up to 2014 (the end of the historical record).
  • Explore ("show me something else" / zoom): We added semantic zoom. If you click and drag horizontally on the chart, it rescales the x and y axes to show just that specific chunk of years in full detail. It transforms the data space, not just the viewport. Double-clicking resets it back to the full 1850–2100 view.
  • Reconfigure ("show me a different arrangement"): Clicking through the different climate variables reconfigures the whole setup. The timeline stays the same, but the data dimension entirely changes, rescaling the y-axis and updating the units.

Data Transformations

We used the CMIP6 dataset, specifically the MPI-ESM1-2-LR model. The original data is stored in massive Zarr files on Google Cloud, and it was extremely difficult to query those spatial arrays dynamically in the browser as it was freezing up. To fix this, we used Python to pre-process everything. We took the historical and projected data cubes, averaged them out globally, and pulled only the values for specific days of the year. Then we exported them as lightweight CSVs so D3 could handle them quickly. We also dropped February 29th since it was too sparse across the climate calendars.

Development Process

We worked iteratively so we could build and test in parallel. Here is how we split up the work:

  • Ananya Wasker: Programmed the interactions, semantic zooming, and set up the overall UI dashboard architecture.

Time & Challenges: In total, we spent about 25 to 30 people-hours on this. The hardest and most time-consuming part was the data wrangling. Navigating Google Cloud authentication, querying the multi-dimensional NetCDF/Zarr files, and formatting the raw CMIP6 spatial data into flat, lightweight CSV structures that D3.js could quickly render without freezing the user's browser was a significant technical hurdle.
